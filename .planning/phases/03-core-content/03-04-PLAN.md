    &lt;h1 class="page-title"&gt;Straight Line Graphs&lt;/h1&gt;

    &lt;h2&gt;Key Facts&lt;/h2&gt;
    &lt;ul&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Equation of a straight line:&lt;/strong&gt; $y = mx + c$, where $m$ is the gradient and $c$ is the $y$-intercept (where the line crosses the $y$-axis).&lt;/li&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Gradient:&lt;/strong&gt; How steep the line is. Positive gradient = slopes up left to right. Negative gradient = slopes down left to right.
        $$m = \frac{\text{rise}}{\text{run}} = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$$
      &lt;/li&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;&lt;strong&gt;y-intercept:&lt;/strong&gt; The value of $y$ when $x = 0$. On the graph this is where the line crosses the vertical axis.&lt;/li&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Plotting a straight line:&lt;/strong&gt; Use the $y$-intercept to mark a starting point on the $y$-axis. Then use the gradient to find two more points — rise over run. Join the points with a ruler.&lt;/li&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Parallel lines:&lt;/strong&gt; Two lines are parallel if and only if they have the same gradient $m$. Example: $y = 3x + 1$ and $y = 3x - 4$ are parallel.&lt;/li&gt;
    &lt;/ul&gt;

    &lt;h2&gt;Worked Example 1: Plotting a Straight Line&lt;/h2&gt;
    &lt;p&gt;Plot the line $y = 2x - 1$. State its gradient and $y$-intercept.&lt;/p&gt;
    &lt;ol&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;Identify gradient and $y$-intercept from the equation $y = 2x - 1$: gradient $m = 2$, $y$-intercept $c = -1$.&lt;/li&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;Mark the $y$-intercept at $(0, -1)$.&lt;/li&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;Use the gradient: from $(0, -1)$, go 1 right and 2 up to reach $(1, 1)$. Repeat to get $(2, 3)$.&lt;/li&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;Build a table of values to confirm:
        &lt;br&gt;When $x = 0$: $y = 2(0) - 1 = -1$, giving point $(0, -1)$
        &lt;br&gt;When $x = 1$: $y = 2(1) - 1 = 1$, giving point $(1, 1)$
        &lt;br&gt;When $x = 2$: $y = 2(2) - 1 = 3$, giving point $(2, 3)$
      &lt;/li&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;Plot the three points and draw a straight line through them.&lt;/li&gt;
    &lt;/ol&gt;
    &lt;p&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Answer: gradient = 2 (rises 2 for every 1 across), $y$-intercept = $-1$.&lt;/strong&gt;&lt;/p&gt;

    &lt;h2&gt;Worked Example 2: Finding the Equation of a Line&lt;/h2&gt;
    &lt;p&gt;Find the equation of the straight line that passes through $(0, 3)$ and $(4, 11)$.&lt;/p&gt;
    &lt;ol&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;Calculate the gradient using the two points:
        $$m = \frac{11 - 3}{4 - 0} = \frac{8}{4} = 2$$
      &lt;/li&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;The first point $(0, 3)$ is on the $y$-axis, so the $y$-intercept is $c = 3$.&lt;/li&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;Write the equation: $y = mx + c = 2x + 3$&lt;/li&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;Check: substitute $x = 4$: $y = 2(4) + 3 = 11$ &amp;checkmark;&lt;/li&gt;
    &lt;/ol&gt;
    &lt;p&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Answer: $y = 2x + 3$&lt;/strong&gt;&lt;/p&gt;

    &lt;h1 class="page-title"&gt;Speed, Distance and Time&lt;/h1&gt;

    &lt;h2&gt;Key Facts&lt;/h2&gt;
    &lt;ul&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;&lt;strong&gt;The three formulae — learn all three:&lt;/strong&gt;
        $$\text{Speed} = \frac{\text{Distance}}{\text{Time}}$$
        $$\text{Distance} = \text{Speed} \times \text{Time}$$
        $$\text{Time} = \frac{\text{Distance}}{\text{Speed}}$$
      &lt;/li&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Units must match:&lt;/strong&gt; If speed is in km/h, distance must be in km and time in hours. If speed is in m/s, distance in metres and time in seconds.&lt;/li&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Converting time:&lt;/strong&gt; Be careful with minutes. 90 minutes $= 1.5$ hours (not 1.9). Divide minutes by 60 to convert to hours.&lt;/li&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Average speed:&lt;/strong&gt; For a whole journey, average speed $=$ total distance $\div$ total time. Do not average the speeds of each part.&lt;/li&gt;
    &lt;/ul&gt;

    &lt;h2&gt;Worked Example 1: Finding Average Speed&lt;/h2&gt;
    &lt;p&gt;A car travels 240 km in 3 hours. Find the average speed.&lt;/p&gt;
    &lt;ol&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;Identify what is given: Distance $= 240$ km, Time $= 3$ hours.&lt;/li&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;Use the speed formula:
        $$\text{Speed} = \frac{\text{Distance}}{\text{Time}} = \frac{240}{3} = 80 \text{ km/h}$$
      &lt;/li&gt;
    &lt;/ol&gt;
    &lt;p&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Answer: 80 km/h&lt;/strong&gt;&lt;/p&gt;

    &lt;h2&gt;Worked Example 2: Finding Distance&lt;/h2&gt;
    &lt;p&gt;A train travels at 60 mph. How far does it travel in 2 hours 30 minutes?&lt;/p&gt;
    &lt;ol&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;Convert 2 hours 30 minutes to hours: $2\text{ h} + \frac{30}{60}\text{ h} = 2 + 0.5 = 2.5$ hours&lt;/li&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;Use the distance formula:
        $$\text{Distance} = \text{Speed} \times \text{Time} = 60 \times 2.5 = 150 \text{ miles}$$
      &lt;/li&gt;
    &lt;/ol&gt;
    &lt;p&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Answer: 150 miles&lt;/strong&gt;&lt;/p&gt;

    &lt;span class="level-badge core"&gt;Core&lt;/span&gt;
    &lt;h1 class="page-title"&gt;Powers and Roots&lt;/h1&gt;

    &lt;h2&gt;Key Facts&lt;/h2&gt;
    &lt;ul&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Powers (indices):&lt;/strong&gt; $a^n$ means $a$ multiplied by itself $n$ times. Example: $2^5 = 2 \times 2 \times 2 \times 2 \times 2 = 32$.&lt;/li&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Square numbers:&lt;/strong&gt; $1, 4, 9, 16, 25, 36, 49, 64, 81, 100, 121, 144, \ldots$ Know these up to $15^2 = 225$.&lt;/li&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Cube numbers:&lt;/strong&gt; $1, 8, 27, 64, 125, 216, \ldots$ ($1^3, 2^3, 3^3, 4^3, 5^3, 6^3, \ldots$)&lt;/li&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Square root:&lt;/strong&gt; $\sqrt{a}$ is the positive number that squares to give $a$. Example: $\sqrt{49} = 7$ because $7^2 = 49$.&lt;/li&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Cube root:&lt;/strong&gt; $\sqrt[3]{a}$ is the number that cubes to give $a$. Example: $\sqrt[3]{27} = 3$ because $3^3 = 27$.&lt;/li&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Using a calculator:&lt;/strong&gt; Use the $x^y$ or $x^{\square}$ button for powers and the $\sqrt{\phantom{x}}$ or $\sqrt[n]{\phantom{x}}$ button for roots.&lt;/li&gt;
    &lt;/ul&gt;

    &lt;h2&gt;Worked Example 1: Mixed Powers and Roots&lt;/h2&gt;
    &lt;p&gt;Without a calculator, evaluate $2^5 + \sqrt{144} - \sqrt[3]{27}$.&lt;/p&gt;
    &lt;ol&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;Evaluate each part:
        &lt;ul&gt;
          &lt;li&gt;$2^5 = 2 \times 2 \times 2 \times 2 \times 2 = 32$&lt;/li&gt;
          &lt;li&gt;$\sqrt{144} = 12$ (since $12^2 = 144$)&lt;/li&gt;
          &lt;li&gt;$\sqrt[3]{27} = 3$ (since $3^3 = 27$)&lt;/li&gt;
        &lt;/ul&gt;
      &lt;/li&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;Combine:
        $$2^5 + \sqrt{144} - \sqrt[3]{27} = 32 + 12 - 3 = 41$$
      &lt;/li&gt;
    &lt;/ol&gt;
    &lt;p&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Answer: 41&lt;/strong&gt;&lt;/p&gt;

    &lt;h2&gt;Worked Example 2: Roots of Decimals&lt;/h2&gt;
    &lt;p&gt;Find $\sqrt{0.49}$ and $\sqrt[3]{0.008}$ without a calculator.&lt;/p&gt;
    &lt;ol&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;For $\sqrt{0.49}$: think of $0.49$ as $\dfrac{49}{100}$.
        $$\sqrt{0.49} = \sqrt{\frac{49}{100}} = \frac{\sqrt{49}}{\sqrt{100}} = \frac{7}{10} = 0.7$$
      &lt;/li&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;For $\sqrt[3]{0.008}$: think of $0.008$ as $\dfrac{8}{1000}$.
        $$\sqrt[3]{0.008} = \sqrt[3]{\frac{8}{1000}} = \frac{\sqrt[3]{8}}{\sqrt[3]{1000}} = \frac{2}{10} = 0.2$$
      &lt;/li&gt;
    &lt;/ol&gt;
    &lt;p&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Answer: $\sqrt{0.49} = 0.7$ and $\sqrt[3]{0.008} = 0.2$&lt;/strong&gt;&lt;/p&gt;

    &lt;h1 class="page-title"&gt;Charts and Data&lt;/h1&gt;

    &lt;h2&gt;Key Facts&lt;/h2&gt;
    &lt;ul&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Bar charts:&lt;/strong&gt; Use a bar chart to compare discrete categories. The height (or length) of each bar represents the frequency. Bars should have equal width.&lt;/li&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Pie charts — sector angle:&lt;/strong&gt; Each sector represents a category. The angle is proportional to the frequency:
        $$\text{Sector angle} = \frac{\text{frequency}}{\text{total}} \times 360^{\circ}$$
      &lt;/li&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Line graphs:&lt;/strong&gt; Used to show how a quantity changes over time. Plot points for each time value and join them with straight lines.&lt;/li&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Scatter graphs — correlation:&lt;/strong&gt; Used to explore whether two variables are related. Describe correlation as positive (both increase together), negative (one increases as the other decreases), or no correlation (no pattern).&lt;/li&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Line of best fit:&lt;/strong&gt; A straight line drawn through the middle of the points on a scatter graph (roughly equal numbers of points above and below). Used to estimate values.&lt;/li&gt;
    &lt;/ul&gt;

    &lt;h2&gt;Worked Example 1: Pie Chart Sector Angle&lt;/h2&gt;
    &lt;p&gt;A survey of 45 pupils asks about their favourite subject. 15 choose Maths, 12 choose English, 9 choose Science, and 9 choose other subjects. Find the sector angle for Maths.&lt;/p&gt;
    &lt;ol&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;Confirm the total: $15 + 12 + 9 + 9 = 45$ &amp;checkmark;&lt;/li&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;Apply the formula:
        $$\text{Sector angle for Maths} = \frac{15}{45} \times 360^{\circ}$$
      &lt;/li&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;Simplify:
        $$= \frac{1}{3} \times 360^{\circ} = 120^{\circ}$$
      &lt;/li&gt;
    &lt;/ol&gt;
    &lt;p&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Answer: The sector angle for Maths is $120^{\circ}$.&lt;/strong&gt;&lt;/p&gt;

    &lt;h2&gt;Worked Example 2: Interpreting a Scatter Graph&lt;/h2&gt;
    &lt;p&gt;A scatter graph plots the number of hours pupils spent revising against their test score (out of 50). The points show that pupils who revised more generally scored higher.&lt;/p&gt;
    &lt;ol&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Describe the correlation:&lt;/strong&gt; As hours of revision increase, test scores also increase. This is &lt;strong&gt;positive correlation&lt;/strong&gt;.&lt;/li&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Interpret in context:&lt;/strong&gt; Pupils who revised for longer tended to achieve higher scores in the test.&lt;/li&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Caution — correlation is not causation:&lt;/strong&gt; A pattern in data does not prove that one thing causes the other. Other factors (e.g. ability, teacher quality) could be involved.&lt;/li&gt;
    &lt;/ol&gt;
    &lt;p&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Answer: Positive correlation — pupils who revised more tended to score higher.&lt;/strong&gt;&lt;/p&gt;
