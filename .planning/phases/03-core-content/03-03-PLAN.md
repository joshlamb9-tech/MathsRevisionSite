    &lt;h2&gt;Key Facts&lt;/h2&gt;
    &lt;ul&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Reflection:&lt;/strong&gt; A shape is flipped in a mirror line. Each point on the image is the same distance from the mirror line as the original point, but on the opposite side. To reflect in the $y$-axis, negate the $x$-coordinate: $(x, y) \to (-x, y)$.&lt;/li&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Rotation:&lt;/strong&gt; A shape is turned through an angle about a centre point. Describe rotation by giving: angle, direction (clockwise or anticlockwise), and centre of rotation.&lt;/li&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Translation:&lt;/strong&gt; A shape is moved without rotating or reflecting. Described using a column vector $\begin{pmatrix}a \\ b\end{pmatrix}$, where $a$ is the horizontal shift (positive = right) and $b$ is the vertical shift (positive = up).&lt;/li&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Enlargement:&lt;/strong&gt; A shape is scaled from a centre of enlargement by a scale factor $k$. If $k = 2$, all lengths double. If $k = \frac{1}{2}$, all lengths halve.&lt;/li&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Congruence:&lt;/strong&gt; Reflections, rotations, and translations produce a congruent image (same shape and size). Enlargements produce a similar but not congruent image.&lt;/li&gt;
    &lt;/ul&gt;

    &lt;h2&gt;Worked Example 1: Reflection in the y-axis&lt;/h2&gt;
    &lt;p&gt;A triangle has vertices at $A(1, 2)$, $B(3, 2)$, and $C(2, 4)$. Reflect it in the $y$-axis. State the new coordinates.&lt;/p&gt;
    &lt;ol&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;To reflect in the $y$-axis, negate the $x$-coordinate of each vertex and keep the $y$-coordinate the same.&lt;/li&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;Apply to each vertex:
        $$A(1, 2) \to A'(-1, 2)$$
        $$B(3, 2) \to B'(-3, 2)$$
        $$C(2, 4) \to C'(-2, 4)$$
      &lt;/li&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;Each image point is the same distance from the $y$-axis as the original, but on the opposite side.&lt;/li&gt;
    &lt;/ol&gt;
    &lt;p&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Answer: $A'(-1, 2)$, $B'(-3, 2)$, $C'(-2, 4)$&lt;/strong&gt;&lt;/p&gt;

    &lt;h1 class="page-title"&gt;Volume and Surface Area&lt;/h1&gt;

    &lt;h2&gt;Key Facts&lt;/h2&gt;
    &lt;ul&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Volume of a cuboid:&lt;/strong&gt;
        $$V = l \times w \times h$$
        where $l$ = length, $w$ = width, $h$ = height. Units are cubic, e.g. $\text{cm}^3$.
      &lt;/li&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Volume of a prism:&lt;/strong&gt;
        $$V = A \times l$$
        where $A$ is the area of the cross-section and $l$ is the length (depth) of the prism.
      &lt;/li&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Surface area of a cuboid:&lt;/strong&gt; The sum of the areas of all six faces. For a cuboid with dimensions $l$, $w$, $h$:
        $$SA = 2(lw + lh + wh)$$
      &lt;/li&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Units:&lt;/strong&gt; Always include correct units. Volume in $\text{cm}^3$ or $\text{m}^3$. Surface area in $\text{cm}^2$ or $\text{m}^2$.&lt;/li&gt;
    &lt;/ul&gt;

    &lt;h2&gt;Worked Example 1: Volume and Surface Area of a Cuboid&lt;/h2&gt;
    &lt;p&gt;A cuboid has dimensions 4 cm &amp;times; 3 cm &amp;times; 5 cm. Find its volume and total surface area.&lt;/p&gt;
    &lt;ol&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;Calculate the volume:
        $$V = 4 \times 3 \times 5 = 60 \text{ cm}^3$$
      &lt;/li&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;Calculate the area of each pair of faces:
        &lt;ul&gt;
          &lt;li&gt;Top and bottom: $4 \times 3 = 12 \text{ cm}^2$ each, so $2 \times 12 = 24 \text{ cm}^2$&lt;/li&gt;
          &lt;li&gt;Front and back: $4 \times 5 = 20 \text{ cm}^2$ each, so $2 \times 20 = 40 \text{ cm}^2$&lt;/li&gt;
          &lt;li&gt;Left and right: $3 \times 5 = 15 \text{ cm}^2$ each, so $2 \times 15 = 30 \text{ cm}^2$&lt;/li&gt;
        &lt;/ul&gt;
      &lt;/li&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;Total surface area:
        $$SA = 24 + 40 + 30 = 94 \text{ cm}^2$$
      &lt;/li&gt;
    &lt;/ol&gt;
    &lt;p&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Answer: Volume = $60 \text{ cm}^3$, Surface area = $94 \text{ cm}^2$&lt;/strong&gt;&lt;/p&gt;

    &lt;h2&gt;Worked Example 2: Volume of a Triangular Prism&lt;/h2&gt;
    &lt;p&gt;A triangular prism has a triangular cross-section with base 6 cm and height 4 cm. The length of the prism is 8 cm. Find the volume.&lt;/p&gt;
    &lt;ol&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;First find the area of the triangular cross-section:
        $$A = \frac{1}{2} \times 6 \times 4 = 12 \text{ cm}^2$$
      &lt;/li&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;Apply the prism formula with the cross-section area and length:
        $$V = A \times l = 12 \times 8 = 96 \text{ cm}^3$$
      &lt;/li&gt;
    &lt;/ol&gt;
    &lt;p&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Answer: Volume = $96 \text{ cm}^3$&lt;/strong&gt;&lt;/p&gt;

    &lt;h1 class="page-title"&gt;Sequences and Nth Term&lt;/h1&gt;

    &lt;h2&gt;Key Facts&lt;/h2&gt;
    &lt;ul&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Arithmetic sequence:&lt;/strong&gt; A sequence where the difference between consecutive terms is constant. This constant difference is called $d$ (the common difference). Example: 3, 7, 11, 15, ... has $d = 4$.&lt;/li&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Finding the nth term:&lt;/strong&gt; For an arithmetic sequence with first term $a$ and common difference $d$:
        $$U_n = dn + (a - d)$$
        A quicker way to remember: $U_n = d \cdot n + c$, where $c$ is the "zeroth term" (the value before the sequence starts, found by subtracting $d$ from the first term).
      &lt;/li&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Using the nth term:&lt;/strong&gt; Substitute any value of $n$ to find that term. For example, $U_{10}$ means the 10th term — substitute $n = 10$.&lt;/li&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Is a value in the sequence?&lt;/strong&gt; Set $U_n$ equal to the value, solve for $n$. If $n$ is a positive integer, the value is in the sequence.&lt;/li&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Square and triangle numbers:&lt;/strong&gt; These are non-arithmetic sequences worth knowing. Square numbers: 1, 4, 9, 16, 25, ... ($U_n = n^2$). Triangle numbers: 1, 3, 6, 10, 15, ... ($U_n = \frac{1}{2}n(n+1)$).&lt;/li&gt;
    &lt;/ul&gt;

    &lt;h2&gt;Worked Example 1: Finding the Nth Term&lt;/h2&gt;
    &lt;p&gt;Find the nth term formula for the sequence 5, 8, 11, 14, ...&lt;/p&gt;
    &lt;ol&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;Find the common difference: $d = 8 - 5 = 3$&lt;/li&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;Find the zeroth term (subtract $d$ from the first term): $a - d = 5 - 3 = 2$&lt;/li&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;Write the formula:
        $$U_n = 3n + 2$$
      &lt;/li&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;Check by substituting $n = 1$: $U_1 = 3(1) + 2 = 5$ &amp;checkmark; and $n = 2$: $U_2 = 3(2) + 2 = 8$ &amp;checkmark;&lt;/li&gt;
    &lt;/ol&gt;
    &lt;p&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Answer: $U_n = 3n + 2$&lt;/strong&gt;&lt;/p&gt;

    &lt;h2&gt;Worked Example 2: Is a Value in the Sequence?&lt;/h2&gt;
    &lt;p&gt;The nth term of a sequence is $U_n = 4n - 3$. Is 100 a term in this sequence?&lt;/p&gt;
    &lt;ol&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;Set the formula equal to 100 and solve for $n$:
        $$4n - 3 = 100$$
      &lt;/li&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;Add 3 to both sides: $4n = 103$&lt;/li&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;Divide by 4:
        $$n = \frac{103}{4} = 25.75$$
      &lt;/li&gt;
      &lt;li&gt;Since $n = 25.75$ is not a positive integer (whole number), 100 is not a term in this sequence.&lt;/li&gt;
    &lt;/ol&gt;
    &lt;p&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Answer: 100 is not a term in the sequence (it falls between the 25th and 26th terms).&lt;/strong&gt;&lt;/p&gt;
