Pythagoras' Theorem

Key Facts

  • Pythagoras' Theorem: For any right-angled triangle with sides $a$ and $b$ and hypotenuse $c$: $$a^2 + b^2 = c^2$$
  • Identifying the hypotenuse: The hypotenuse ($c$) is the side OPPOSITE the right angle — always the longest side. Identify it first before applying the theorem.
  • Finding the hypotenuse: $$c = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}$$
  • Finding a shorter side: Rearrange the theorem to make the unknown the subject: $$a = \sqrt{c^2 - b^2}$$
  • Pythagorean triples — sets of whole numbers that satisfy $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$. Recognising these saves calculation time:
    • 3 – 4 – 5 (and multiples: 6-8-10, 9-12-15, …)
    • 5 – 12 – 13
    • 8 – 15 – 17
    If the side lengths are multiples of a triple, you know it is a right-angled triangle.

Worked Example 1: Finding the Hypotenuse

A right-angled triangle has sides of 6 cm and 8 cm. Find the hypotenuse.

  1. Identify the hypotenuse $c$ — it is the side opposite the right angle (the unknown here). Label the sides $a = 6$ and $b = 8$.
  2. Apply Pythagoras' Theorem: $$c^2 = a^2 + b^2$$
  3. Substitute the known values: $$c^2 = 6^2 + 8^2 = 36 + 64 = 100$$
  4. Take the square root: $$c = \sqrt{100} = 10 \text{ cm}$$

Answer: The hypotenuse is 10 cm. (This is the 6-8-10 triple — a multiple of the 3-4-5 triple.)

Worked Example 2: Finding a Shorter Side

A right-angled triangle has a hypotenuse of 13 cm and one leg of 5 cm. Find the missing leg.

  1. Identify: hypotenuse $c = 13$ cm (the longest side, opposite the right angle); known leg $b = 5$ cm; find leg $a$.
  2. Rearrange the theorem to make $a^2$ the subject: $$a^2 = c^2 - b^2$$
  3. Substitute the known values: $$a^2 = 13^2 - 5^2 = 169 - 25 = 144$$
  4. Take the square root: $$a = \sqrt{144} = 12 \text{ cm}$$

Answer: The missing leg is 12 cm. (The classic 5-12-13 Pythagorean triple.)
