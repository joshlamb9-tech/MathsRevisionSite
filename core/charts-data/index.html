Key Facts

  • Bar charts: Use a bar chart to compare discrete categories. The height (or length) of each bar represents the frequency. Bars should have equal width.
  • Pie charts — sector angle: Each sector represents a category. The angle is proportional to the frequency: $$\text{Sector angle} = \frac{\text{frequency}}{\text{total}} \times 360^{\circ}$$
  • Line graphs: Used to show how a quantity changes over time. Plot points for each time value and join them with straight lines.
  • Scatter graphs — correlation: Used to explore whether two variables are related. Describe correlation as positive (both increase together), negative (one increases as the other decreases), or no correlation (no pattern).
  • Line of best fit: A straight line drawn through the middle of the points on a scatter graph (roughly equal numbers of points above and below). Used to estimate values.

Worked Example 1: Pie Chart Sector Angle

A survey of 45 pupils asks about their favourite subject. 15 choose Maths, 12 choose English, 9 choose Science, and 9 choose other subjects. Find the sector angle for Maths.

  1. Confirm the total: $15 + 12 + 9 + 9 = 45$ ✓
  2. Apply the formula: $$\text{Sector angle for Maths} = \frac{15}{45} \times 360^{\circ}$$
  3. Simplify: $$= \frac{1}{3} \times 360^{\circ} = 120^{\circ}$$

Answer: The sector angle for Maths is $120^{\circ}$.

Worked Example 2: Interpreting a Scatter Graph

A scatter graph plots the number of hours pupils spent revising against their test score (out of 50). The points show that pupils who revised more generally scored higher.

  1. Describe the correlation: As hours of revision increase, test scores also increase. This is positive correlation.
  2. Interpret in context: Pupils who revised for longer tended to achieve higher scores in the test.
  3. Caution — correlation is not causation: A pattern in data does not prove that one thing causes the other. Other factors (e.g. ability, teacher quality) could be involved.

Answer: Positive correlation — pupils who revised more tended to score higher.
